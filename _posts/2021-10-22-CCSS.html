What are the Common Core State Standards?

The Common Core State Standards are a set of academic standards for English Language Arts and Mathematics, grades kindergarten through twelve, that provide benchmarks for what students should be able to do in each content area and grade level.

Beginning in 2009, members of the National Governors Association Center for Best Practices (NGA Center) and the Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO) developed the set of standards, the final version of which was published in June of 2010.

Since their publication, the standards have been voluntarily adopted by “forty-one states, the District of Columbia, four territories, and the department of Defense Education Activity.” While the Common Core State Standards do not dictate a particular course of reading, nor are they affiliated with any particular textbooks, they do have a concern with what Standard 10 refers to as “Range, Quality, & Complexity” of texts in the classroom.

Text Complexity

Under the section, “Measuring Text Complexity: Three Factors,” as well as in Appendix A, the factors considered and the rationale behind “text complexity” are detailed.

Citing a 2006 report from ACT, Inc., Reading Between the Lines, Appendix A says:

“The clear, alarming picture that emerges from the evidence, briefly summarized below, is that while the reading demands of college, workforce training programs, and citizenship have held steady or risen over the past fifty years or so, K–12 texts have, if anything, become less demanding. This finding is the impetus behind the Standards’ strong emphasis on increasing text complexity as a key requirement in reading.”

After establishing the rationale behind text complexity as foundational to their approach, the CCSS then details their methodology concerning text complexity saying:

“To help redress the situation described above, the Standards define a three-part model for determining how easy or difficult a particular text is to read as well as grade-by-grade specifications for increasing text complexity in successive years of schooling (Reading standard 10). These are to be used together with grade-specific standards that require increasing sophistication in students’ reading comprehension ability (Reading standards 1–9). The Standards thus approach the intertwined issues of what and how student read.”

The three measures are:

1. Qualitative dimensions of text complexity: In the Standards, qualitative dimensions and qualitative factors refer to those aspects of text complexity best measured or only measurable by an attentive human reader, such as levels of meaning or purpose; structure; language conventionality and clarity; and knowledge demands.
2. Quantitative dimensions of text complexity: The terms quantitative dimensions and quantitative factors refer to those aspects of text complexity, such as word length or frequency, sentence length, and text cohesion, that are difficult if not impossible for a human reader to evaluate efficiently, especially in long texts, and are thus today typically measured by computer software.
3. Reader and task considerations : While the prior two elements of the model focus on the inherent complexity of text, variables specific to particular readers (such as motivation, knowledge, and experiences) and to particular tasks (such as purpose and the complexity of the task assigned and the questions posed) must also be considered when determining whether a text is appropriate for a given student. Such assessments are best made by teachers employing their professional judgment, experience, and knowledge of their students and the subject.

These measures are meant to be used at the local level to help school leaders and teachers select appropriate texts for their students. While I’m sure there is much that could be critiqued in the methodology outlined above, my interest lies in the material that comprises Appendix B: Text Exemplars and Sample Performance Tasks.

Although the Standards are careful to say:

“The choices should serve as useful guideposts in helping educators select texts of similar complexity, quality, and range for their own classrooms. They expressly do not represent a partial or complete reading list.”

My own anecdotal experience in the field demonstrates that many school leaders, do, in fact, implement the exemplar texts as the reading list for their English Language Arts Curriculum.

Barbara Moss, writing for Voices From The Middle, shares my skepticism that the Common Core State Standards exemplar lists will not become the de facto new curriculum saying, “Many districts have rushed to buy these ti- tles, sometimes assuming these books may form the basis for test items that will ultimately appear on national assessments…Publishers are already using the books as a template for what to include in their textbooks."

In fact, 68% of the texts on the Common Core State Standards Exemplar Texts list are also included in one of the textbooks I reviewed as part of this project.

Mapping the Common Core

The rest of this page explores the Common Core State Standards exemplar texts, looking at authors’ ethnicity, gender, and birthplace.

I’ve used several historical maps from the David Rumsey Historical Map Collection to help visualize the temporality and spatiality of the texts that the Common Core State Standards holds up as exemplars. Using maps that are cotemporaneous with the time of each text’s publication date helps to better understand the version of the world the Common Core State Standards exemplar text collection presents to students.

Explore the maps and visualizations below and ask yourself, how does the vision of the world these texts create align with students’ lived experiences of the world both inside of and outside of the classroom? I invite you to compare the visualizations below with the visualizations portraying LAUSD and NYDOE district demographics.

The 19th Century
