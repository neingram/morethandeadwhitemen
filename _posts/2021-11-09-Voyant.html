To create the following visualizations, I used Voyant, a "web-based text reading and analysis environment," created by Geoffrey Rockwell and the late St√©fan Sinclair, using the dataset I developed for this project.

I chose to use Voyant's tag-cloud feature, Cirrus, which uses size to represent the frequency of each specific term.

Looking at the visualization above of the authors' names, you can clearly see that the most prominent name dispalyed is "William Shakespare." Two other names visually jump out, "James" and "John." Thus, despite the push for greater curriculum diversity as connected to the call for "multiculturalism" in the 1960s, 1970s, and 1990s, it's striking to note the continued prominence of both William Shakespeare as a central author in high school ELA curricula, as well as the dominance of typical Anglo male names, "James" and "John."

Visualizing the titles of the assigned texts provides insight into the link between public educaiton and the formation of citizens in the United States.

The top three words in the visualization above, "America," "address," and "dream," speak to the enduring legacy of this connection between public education and civic education, highlighting underlying ideological themes in contemporary public school ELA education. Given this connection between citizenship and the stories studied in the context of high school ELA classrooms, questions such as, "Whose stories are told?", "Who is included in the curriculum?", "To what extent?" have implications beyond the walls of the classroom. The answers to these questions illustrate questions of national belonging-- who does or does not count as a citizen?
